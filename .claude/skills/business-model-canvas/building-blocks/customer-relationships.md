# Customer Relationships (CR)

> *"The Customer Relationships Building Block describes the types of relationships a company establishes with specific Customer Segments."*
> — Osterwalder & Pigneur, p. 28

## Definition

A company should clearly define the type of relationship it wants to establish with each customer segment. Relationships can range from personal to fully automated. Customer relationships deeply influence the overall customer experience.

## Key questions

- What type of relationship does each customer segment expect us to establish and maintain?
- Which ones have we established?
- How costly are they?
- How are they integrated with the rest of our business model?

## Motivations behind relationship choices

- **Customer acquisition** — attracting new customers
- **Customer retention** — keeping existing customers
- **Boosting sales (upselling)** — selling more to existing customers

*Example: Early mobile network operators focused aggressively on acquisition (free phones). As the market became saturated, they shifted focus to retention and increasing average revenue per customer.*

## Categories of customer relationships

### Personal assistance
Based on human interaction. The customer can communicate with a real representative — during the sales process or after purchase. Can happen in person at the point of sale, through call centers, by email, or other means.

### Dedicated personal assistance
A customer representative dedicated to one individual client. The deepest and most intimate relationship type — typically developed over a long period. Example: private banking, key account managers.

### Self-service
The company maintains no direct relationship with customers. It provides all the means necessary for customers to help themselves.

### Automated services
Combines self-service with automated processes. Personal online profiles give customers access to customized services. Automated systems can recognize individual customers and offer relevant information (e.g. book and movie recommendations, like Amazon).

### Communities
The company builds user communities where customers can exchange knowledge and solve each other's problems. Also helps the company better understand its customers. Example: GlaxoSmithKline launched a private online community for alli (a weight-loss product) to deepen its understanding of overweight adults' challenges.

### Co-creation
Customers contribute to creating value. Example: Amazon invites customers to write reviews. YouTube lets users create content. Some companies involve customers in designing new products.

## Questions to ask

1. What does the customer expect — personal contact or self-service?
2. Is this a transactional or long-term relationship?
3. Who initiates contact — the customer or the company?
4. How costly is the relationship model to maintain?
5. Does the relationship type support the value proposition? (Premium VP should have a premium relationship)

## Connections to other building blocks

- **CS**: Different segments expect different relationship types
- **VP**: Relationship type should support and reinforce the value proposition
- **CH**: Channels and relationships overlap — both shape the customer experience
- **C$**: Personal relationships are costly; automated ones are scalable
