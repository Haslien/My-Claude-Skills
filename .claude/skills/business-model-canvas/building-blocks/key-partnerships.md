# Key Partnerships (KP)

> *"The Key Partnerships Building Block describes the network of suppliers and partners that make the business model work."*
> — Osterwalder & Pigneur, p. 38

## Definition

Companies forge partnerships for many reasons, and partnerships are becoming a cornerstone of many business models. Companies create alliances to optimize their business models, reduce risk, or acquire resources.

## Key questions

- Who are our key partners?
- Who are our key suppliers?
- Which key resources are we acquiring from partners?
- Which key activities do partners perform?

## Four types of partnerships

| Type | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **Strategic alliance** | Between non-competitors |
| **Coopetition** | Strategic partnership between competitors |
| **Joint venture** | Collaboration to develop new business |
| **Buyer-supplier relationship** | To assure reliable supplies |

## Three motivations for partnerships

### 1. Optimization and economy of scale
The most basic form — optimizing the allocation of resources and activities. It is illogical for a company to own all resources or perform every activity by itself. These partnerships are usually formed to reduce costs and often involve outsourcing or sharing infrastructure.

**Example:** An insurer chooses to rely on independent brokers to sell its policies rather than develop its own sales force.

### 2. Reduction of risk and uncertainty
Partnerships can help reduce risk in a competitive environment characterized by uncertainty. Even competitors can form a strategic alliance in one area while competing in another.

**Example:** Blu-ray — developed by a group of the world's leading consumer electronics, PC, and media manufacturers. The group cooperated to bring Blu-ray to market, yet individual members competed in selling their own Blu-ray products.

### 3. Acquisition of particular resources and activities
Few companies own all the resources or perform all the activities their business model requires. They extend their own capabilities by relying on other firms for particular resources or activities. Can be motivated by needs to acquire knowledge, licenses, or access to customers.

**Examples:**
- A mobile phone manufacturer licenses an operating system rather than developing one in-house
- An insurer uses independent brokers instead of building its own sales force

## Questions to ask

1. Which activities are not core competencies and should be outsourced?
2. Which resources are too expensive or difficult to acquire alone?
3. Who already has the relationship, expertise, or distribution we need?
4. What is the risk of depending on a particular partner (supplier lock-in)?
5. Is there an opportunity to collaborate with a competitor (coopetition)?

## Connections to other building blocks

- **KR**: Resources we don't own can be sourced through partners
- **KA**: Activities we don't perform ourselves can be done by partners
- **CH**: Partner channels are a form of key partnership
- **C$**: Partnerships can reduce costs but also create dependencies
