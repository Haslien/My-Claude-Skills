# Key Resources (KR)

> *"The Key Resources Building Block describes the most important assets required to make a business model work."*
> — Osterwalder & Pigneur, p. 34

## Definition

Every business model requires key resources. These resources allow the company to create and deliver its value proposition, reach markets, maintain relationships with customer segments, and earn revenues. Key resources can be owned or leased by the company, or acquired from key partners.

## Key questions

- What key resources do our value propositions require?
- Our distribution channels?
- Our customer relationships?
- Our revenue streams?

## Categories of key resources

### Physical
Physical assets such as manufacturing facilities, buildings, vehicles, machines, systems, point-of-sales systems, and distribution networks.

**Examples:**
- Wal-Mart: enormous global store network and logistics infrastructure
- Amazon: extensive IT, warehouse, and logistics infrastructure
- Manufacturing companies: capital-intensive production facilities

### Intellectual
Brands, proprietary knowledge, patents and copyrights, partnerships, and customer databases. Difficult to build but highly valuable once established.

**Examples:**
- Nike and Sony: brand as a key resource
- Microsoft and SAP: software and IP developed over many years
- Qualcomm: patented chip designs that generate licensing fees

### Human
Every enterprise requires human resources, but some business models are particularly people-dependent — especially knowledge-intensive and creative industries.

**Examples:**
- Novartis: relies on an army of experienced scientists and a large, skilled sales force
- Consulting firms: problem-solving requires talented people

### Financial
Cash, lines of credit, stock option pools for hiring key employees.

**Example:**
- Ericsson: can borrow capital and use a portion to provide vendor financing to equipment customers — ensuring orders go to Ericsson rather than competitors.

## Questions to ask

1. Which resources are so critical that the business model cannot function without them?
2. Which resources are unique/proprietary — and what can be bought or leased externally?
3. Are the resources built internally, acquired, or sourced from partners?
4. Which resources are hardest to obtain or replicate?
5. What happens to the business model if we lose a key resource?

## Connections to other building blocks

- **VP**: KR must make it possible to create and deliver the value proposition
- **KA**: Resources and activities are tightly linked — resources enable activities
- **KP**: Resources we don't own can be sourced through key partners
- **C$**: Key resources are typically among the largest cost drivers
