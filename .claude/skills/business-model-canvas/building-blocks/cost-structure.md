# Cost Structure (C$)

> *"The Cost Structure describes all costs incurred to operate a business model."*
> — Osterwalder & Pigneur, p. 40

## Definition

The cost structure describes the most important costs incurred while operating a particular business model. Creating and delivering value, maintaining customer relationships, and generating revenue all incur costs. These can be calculated relatively easily after defining key resources, key activities, and key partnerships.

## Key questions

- What are the most important costs inherent in our business model?
- Which key resources are most expensive?
- Which key activities are most expensive?

## Two broad classes

### Cost-driven
Focuses on minimizing costs wherever possible. Goal: the leanest possible cost structure, low-price value propositions, maximum automation, and extensive outsourcing.

**Examples:** Southwest, easyJet, Ryanair — low-cost airlines with business models built entirely around a low cost structure.

### Value-driven
Less concerned with the cost implications of a particular business model design — instead focuses on value creation. Premium value propositions and a high degree of personalized service characterize these models.

**Example:** Luxury hotels with lavish facilities and exclusive services.

*Most business models fall somewhere between these two extremes.*

## Characteristics of cost structures

### Fixed costs
Costs that remain the same regardless of the volume of goods or services produced.

**Examples:** Salaries, rents, physical manufacturing facilities. Manufacturing companies are typically characterized by a high proportion of fixed costs.

### Variable costs
Costs that vary proportionally with the volume of goods or services produced.

**Example:** Music festivals — characterized by a high proportion of variable costs.

### Economies of scale
Cost advantages a business enjoys as its output expands. Larger companies benefit from lower bulk purchase rates. Average cost per unit falls as output rises.

### Economies of scope
Cost advantages due to a larger scope of operations. In a large enterprise, the same marketing activities or distribution channels may support multiple products.

## Questions to ask

1. Is the business model primarily cost-driven or value-driven?
2. What is the ratio of fixed to variable costs?
3. Which KA and KR drive the largest costs?
4. Are there opportunities for economies of scale as the business grows?
5. Which costs are critical for delivering the VP — and which are overhead?
6. Are there costs that could be eliminated without negatively affecting the VP?

## Connections to other building blocks

- **KR**: Key resources are typically among the largest cost drivers
- **KA**: Key activities are typically among the largest cost drivers
- **KP**: Partnerships can reduce costs (e.g. shared infrastructure)
- **R$**: Revenue minus costs = profit — these two define the business's viability
