# Value Propositions (VP)

> *"The Value Propositions Building Block describes the bundle of products and services that create value for a specific Customer Segment."*
> — Osterwalder & Pigneur, p. 22

## Definition

The value proposition is *the reason why customers choose your company over another*. It solves a customer problem or satisfies a customer need. Each VP is a bundle of benefits a company offers a specific customer segment — both quantitative (price, speed) and qualitative (design, experience).

## Key questions

- What value do we deliver to the customer?
- Which customer problem are we helping to solve?
- Which customer needs are we satisfying?
- What bundle of products and services are we offering to each customer segment?

## Elements that create value

### Newness
Satisfies an entirely new set of needs customers didn't previously perceive — often technology-driven. Example: mobile phones creating a whole new industry around mobile communication.

### Performance
Improving product or service performance. Traditionally common, but has its limits — faster PCs no longer drive corresponding growth in demand.

### Customization
Tailoring products and services to individual customers or segments. Mass customization and co-creation are growing in relevance.

### "Getting the job done"
Value created by helping a customer complete a specific job. Example: Rolls-Royce manufactures and services jet engines so airlines can focus on running their business — airlines pay per engine flying hour.

### Design
Important but difficult to measure. A product may stand out through superior design. Particularly relevant in fashion and consumer electronics.

### Brand / Status
Customers find value in using and displaying a specific brand. E.g. Rolex = wealth. Underground brands = belonging.

### Price
Same value at a lower price. Has significant implications for the rest of the business model. Example: Southwest, easyJet, Ryanair (low-cost carriers). Tata's Nano car.

### Cost reduction
Helping customers reduce their own costs. Example: Salesforce.com — businesses don't have to buy, install, and manage CRM software themselves.

### Risk reduction
Customers value reducing risk when purchasing. Example: one-year service guarantee on a used car; SLA guarantees on outsourced IT services.

### Accessibility
Making products/services available to customers who previously lacked access. Example: NetJets (fractional private jet ownership); mutual funds (diversified portfolios for people with modest wealth).

### Convenience / Usability
Making things easier to use. Example: iPod + iTunes gave customers unprecedented convenience in finding, buying, downloading, and listening to music.

## Questions to ask

1. What does the customer *really* buy — what is the underlying job to be done?
2. What are the customer's pain points today?
3. What do competitors do, and what do we do differently or better?
4. Is the VP innovative (new category) or an improvement on an existing offer?
5. Is the VP differentiated quantitatively or qualitatively?
6. Is there one VP for all segments, or distinct VPs per segment?

## Connections to other building blocks

- **CS**: Each VP must address the specific needs and problems of a segment
- **CH**: VP is delivered through channels — channels must match customer expectations
- **CR**: Relationship type should support the VP (e.g. premium VP → dedicated personal assistance)
- **R$**: Customers pay for VP — price level and model should reflect perceived value
- **KA + KR**: Activities and resources must make it possible to deliver the VP
