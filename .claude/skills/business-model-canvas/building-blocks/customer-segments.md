# Customer Segments (CS)

> *"The Customer Segments Building Block defines the different groups of people or organizations an enterprise aims to reach and serve."*
> — Osterwalder & Pigneur, p. 20

## Definition

Customers are the heart of any business model. Without (profitable) customers, no company can survive for long. A business must make a conscious decision about which segments to serve — and which to ignore. Once that decision is made, the model can be carefully designed around a strong understanding of specific customer needs.

## Key questions

- For whom are we creating value?
- Who are our most important customers?

## When do groups constitute separate segments?

A group is a distinct segment if at least one of the following applies:
- They require a distinct offer (VP)
- They are reached through different channels (CH)
- They require different types of relationships (CR)
- They have substantially different profitabilities
- They are willing to pay for different aspects of the offer

## Types of customer segments

### Mass market
The business model makes no distinction between different customer segments. VP, CH, and CR all focus on one large group with broadly similar needs and problems. Example: consumer electronics.

### Niche market
The model is tailored to a specific, specialized segment. VP, CH, and CR are all tuned to the niche's requirements. Common in supplier-buyer relationships (e.g. auto parts manufacturers serving major car makers).

### Segmented
The model distinguishes between segments with *slightly* different needs. Example: a retail bank serving customers with assets under $100K differently from those with over $500K.

### Diversified
The model serves two *completely unrelated* segments with very different needs and problems. Example: Amazon — retail for consumers *and* cloud computing (AWS) for businesses.

### Multi-sided platforms (multi-sided markets)
The model serves two or more *interdependent* segments simultaneously. Example: a credit card company needs *both* cardholders *and* merchants. A free newspaper needs *both* readers *and* advertisers. (See p. 76 of the book.)

## Questions to ask

1. Who is the primary customer — and is that the same person who pays?
2. What do customers in this segment have in common (demographics, behavior, needs)?
3. Is there more than one segment? What distinguishes them?
4. Which segments are most profitable?
5. Which segments are we deliberately *not* serving?

## Connections to other building blocks

- **VP**: Each segment should have at least one distinct value proposition
- **CH**: Different segments may require different channels
- **CR**: Different segments may expect different relationship types
- **R$**: Different segments often pay in different ways and at different price points
